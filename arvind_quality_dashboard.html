05 · The Core Finding

Process Variation > Cotton Variation

The same cotton blend (ACM1-28, Group 328) was tested 4 times over different days. With identical cotton input, Total Imperfections ranged from 85 to 130. This gap cannot be explained by cotton — it points to machine state, ambient conditions, or shift effects.

06 · Interpretation

Key Findings & Honest Caveats

FINDING 01
Cotton input is remarkably uniform
All 8 fibre properties have CV% below 1.9% across 53 groups. Fibre length ranges just 29.34–29.58 mm (CV 0.16%). Micronaire ranges 4.32–4.53 (CV 1.02%). Arvind's sourcing from VAR-A and BCI VAR-A premium cotton is tightly controlled — a quality management success, but it removes the variation needed for statistical analysis.
FINDING 02
Yarn quality varies far more than cotton
Total Imperfections CV = 17.9%. Thin places CV = 60%. These swamp the cotton input signal completely. The ratio of yarn noise to cotton signal is roughly 10:1 for imperfections, making attribution nearly impossible with this dataset.
FINDING 03
MIC is the most directional cotton driver
Higher micronaire (coarser fibre) correlates with more imperfections (r = +0.46). The period mean MIC of 4.47 is slightly above the optimal range of 3.5–4.2 for 40s count yarn. Sourcing finer BCI lots could theoretically reduce imperfections — but this hypothesis needs more varied data to confirm.
FINDING 04
Yarn is failing imperfection tolerances — not a cotton problem
10 of 13 tests exceed the Total Imperfections limit of 85/km. Since identical cotton produces widely varying imperfection counts across test dates, the root cause is almost certainly process-side: machine maintenance state, spindle wear, ring/traveller condition, or environmental factors.
FINDING 05
SFI trending lower — a positive signal
Short Fibre Index has declined from 7.2 in late March to 6.6 by late April. Lower SFI generally means cleaner yarn and fewer neps. This could reflect supplier lot rotation, or a slight seasonal improvement in cotton quality as new-crop lots enter the blend.
CAVEAT
What this data cannot tell us
With n=8 matched pairs from only 3 unique blends, no correlation clears p < 0.05. To test whether cotton drives yarn quality here, you need either a longer window with more blend diversity, or a designed experiment that deliberately varies one property (e.g. MIC) while controlling others. The current data answers a different question: process variation is the dominant driver of yarn quality in this window.
Recommendation: Audit machine ACM1 for spindle/ring/traveller wear and check environmental logs (temperature, humidity) against the high-imperfection test dates (Apr 9, Apr 11, Apr 27, Apr 30). The cotton is not the problem. If imperfection failures persist after process inspection, consider requesting a wider MIC range from procurement to create testable variation for future analysis.
